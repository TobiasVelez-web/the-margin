I'm 16. Should I open a Roth IRA? Here's what I found out.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

Every adult I've talked to about money says some version of the same thing: I wish I started earlier. At some point I stopped writing that off as something people just say and actually looked at the math. Turns out they're right in a way that's kind of unsettling once you see it.

What a Roth IRA is

A Roth IRA is a retirement account where you invest money you've already paid taxes on. The deal is: pay taxes now, never pay them again on any of the growth. If you put money in at 16 and it compounds for 49 years, you pull all of it out tax-free at retirement. The government doesn't get another cut. For someone who's decades away from needing that money, that's a genuinely good arrangement.

In 2026 you can contribute up to ,000 per year, but only if you have earned income. A job, babysitting, tutoring, mowing lawns, any of that counts. Birthday money doesn't. The limit is whatever you actually earned, up to ,000. You can open one at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard with a parent as co-owner until you turn 18. No minimum balance. Takes about 30 minutes online.

The math that got my attention

,000 invested at 16, assuming 7% average annual return, becomes about 5,000 by age 65. The same ,000 invested at 30 becomes ,400. Same money, same return rate, three times less because you waited 14 years. The variable that matters most isn't how much you invest or how well you pick stocks. It's just time.

What it looks like with actual contributions

The single investment example is dramatic but not how most people do it. The more realistic scenario is putting in a few hundred to a few thousand dollars per year from a job. Even small amounts compounded over 49 years become serious money.

The catch

You need earned income. If you make ,800 at your job this summer, that's your max contribution for the year, not ,000. No job means no contribution. This is actually a decent reason to get a part-time job if you can, beyond just having spending money. Even 00 or 00 invested at 16 matters more than people think.

Is it worth doing at 16

If you have a job and can put even a few hundred dollars away, yes. Not because it makes you rich tomorrow but because those dollars get 49 years of compounding that your 30-year-old self won't have access to. The account also makes early withdrawals annoying on purpose. You can't touch the earnings until 59 and a half without a penalty. That turns out to be a feature. Future you will be grateful present you couldn't spend it on something stupid.
