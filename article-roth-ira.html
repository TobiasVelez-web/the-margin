I'm 16. Should I open a Roth IRA? Here is what I actually found.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

Every adult I talk to about money eventually says the same thing: I wish I had started earlier. For a long time I treated that as generic advice, the kind that sounds wise but doesn't actually tell you what to do. Then I ran the numbers on it. It turns out to be one of the most precisely demonstrable claims in personal finance, and the mechanism is simple enough that the fact most teenagers never hear a clear explanation of it is genuinely strange.

What a Roth IRA is and why the structure matters

A Roth IRA is a retirement account funded with money you have already paid income tax on. The trade is: pay your taxes now, and all growth inside the account from that point forward is permanently tax-free. No capital gains tax on decades of appreciation. No income tax on withdrawals in retirement. The government takes its cut once, upfront, and after that the compounding belongs entirely to you.

For a 16-year-old, that structure is about as favorable as it gets. You are paying taxes at what is almost certainly the lowest income tax rate of your life. The growth that follows happens inside a tax-free wrapper for the next 49 years. And unlike a traditional IRA, there are no required minimum distributions at retirement, no forced withdrawals, no future tax event on the gains. Time is the primary asset here, and at 16 you have more of it than you ever will again.

In 2026 the contribution limit is $7,000 per year. The requirement is earned income: wages from a job, self-employment, tutoring, mowing lawns, any documented paid work. Passive income, gifts, and allowances do not count. Your contribution is capped at whichever is smaller, the $7,000 limit or your actual earned income for the year. You can open one at Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab with a parent as custodian until age 18. There is no minimum balance. It takes roughly 30 minutes.

The math that changed how I thought about this

$1,000 invested at 16, compounding at 7% annually (the S&P 500's inflation-adjusted historical average), reaches approximately $15,000 by age 65. That same $1,000 invested at 30 reaches around $5,400. Same money. Same return rate. Fourteen fewer years of compounding cuts the outcome by roughly two thirds. The difference is not about skill or risk tolerance or market timing. It is purely about when you started.

What consistent contributions actually produce

The single-investment example illustrates the mechanism, but the more realistic scenario is putting in a few hundred to a few thousand dollars per year from a job. $2,000 a year invested from age 16 in a basic S&P 500 index fund grows to roughly $780,000 by age 65. No stock picking, no market timing, no skill required beyond consistency. $5,000 a year from age 16 gets you close to $2 million. What is notable about those numbers is that the majority of the final balance is not money you contributed. It is returns on returns on returns, accumulated over decades inside a tax-free account.

The tax-free compounding is what separates a Roth from a regular brokerage account. At $780,000, the capital gains taxes owed in a taxable account at withdrawal could easily run $100,000 to $200,000 depending on your bracket and the composition of the gains. In a Roth, that stays in your account. The tax wrapper is not a minor detail. It is a significant portion of the eventual value.

The restriction that is actually a feature

You cannot withdraw the earnings before age 59 and a half without paying taxes and a 10% penalty. This sounds like a limitation, and technically it is. But it is also what makes the account work as intended. It makes it genuinely inconvenient to raid decades of compound growth for a near-term expense. One important nuance: you can withdraw your contributions at any time without penalty. The restriction applies specifically to the earnings, not the principal. The account is not inaccessible in a genuine emergency. It is structured to make casual withdrawals unattractive, which turns out to be exactly what you want from a retirement vehicle.

Whether it actually makes sense to open one at 16

If you have any earned income at all, yes. The question is not really whether the Roth IRA is a good account structure. It is whether the dollars you put in at 16 are meaningfully more valuable than the dollars you put in at 25 or 30. The math says they are, substantially. The first dollars you invest are the ones that get the longest compounding runway. They are the most powerful dollars you will ever have access to from a pure time-value perspective, and most people let them pass without doing anything because nobody explained this clearly when they had the chance to act on it. The adults who tell you they wish they had started earlier are not making up a cliche. They are telling you something specific and true.
