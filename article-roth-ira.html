I'm 16. Should I open a Roth IRA? Here's what I found out.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

Most adults I talk to say the same thing when the topic of retirement comes up: "I wish I started saving earlier." That got me wondering — can you actually start investing for retirement as a teenager? Turns out, you can. And the math of doing it early is genuinely surprising.

What a Roth IRA actually is

A Roth IRA is an individual retirement account that lets you invest after-tax money now, so your withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. You pay taxes on the money going in — but everything it grows into over the next 40 or 50 years comes out tax-free. That's the deal.

In 2026, you can contribute up to $7,000 per year — but only if you have earned income. A real job, babysitting, tutoring, mowing lawns, anything paid work counts. Gift money from your parents or birthday cash doesn't. The rule is: you can contribute up to however much you actually earned that year, up to the $7,000 limit.

You can open a custodial Roth IRA with a parent at Fidelity, Charles Schwab, or Vanguard — no minimum balance, takes about 30 minutes online. You need a Social Security number, proof of earnings, and a parent for the custodial setup.

The math of starting early

This is where it gets genuinely interesting. Compound interest — where your interest earns interest — is brutally powerful over long time horizons. The earlier you start, the more decades of compounding you get. Same $1,000 investment, same 7% return — but starting at 16 instead of 30 results in almost 3x more money at retirement. Starting at 40 gives you less than a fifth of what you'd have starting at 16. That gap is entirely time.

The numbers: $1,000 invested at 16 grows to about $15,000 by 65. The same $1,000 invested at 30 reaches about $5,400. At 40, you're looking at around $2,700. Same money, same return, completely different outcome — the only variable is time.

What consistent contributions actually look like

The single-investment example is dramatic, but the real power is consistent yearly contributions. Even small amounts, invested every year from a summer job, add up to something most adults would be shocked by.

Contributing $2,000/year from age 16 — roughly a summer job's worth of savings — could grow to about $780,000 by retirement. That's with no changes to lifestyle, no stock picking, just a basic index fund.

How to actually open one

The most straightforward option is a custodial Roth IRA, which means a parent or guardian co-owns it with you until you turn 18. Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Vanguard all offer these with no minimum balance. You'll need your Social Security number, a record of your earned income (a W-2 or even a note of what you made babysitting), and a parent's information for the setup. The whole process takes about 30 minutes online. Once it's open, put the money into a broad index fund — something like a total stock market fund or an S&P 500 fund — and leave it alone.

The catch — and it's a real one

You need earned income. If you make $1,800 at a summer job this year, the maximum you can contribute is $1,800 — not $7,000. And if you have no job income, you can't contribute at all. This is actually a good reason to get a part-time job if you can — even a few hundred dollars invested at 16 is worth more than most people realize.

My take

I'll be honest — before I researched this, I thought Roth IRAs were for adults with "real" jobs. But the math makes it pretty clear: even $500 or $1,000 from a summer job, put into a Roth at 16, is genuinely worth doing. Not because it'll make you rich overnight, but because you're giving yourself 49 years of compounding that your 30-year-old self won't have. The only question is whether you have the discipline to not touch it. Given that you can't easily withdraw the earnings until 59½ anyway, the account basically enforces that discipline for you.
