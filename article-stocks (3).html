Personal Finance

The stock market feels abstract. Here's why I still pay attention.

Tobias Velez·February 2026·5 min read

Every day, news alerts tell you whether the S&P 500 went up or down as if that were a direct measure of how everyone is doing. For most high school students, these numbers feel completely distant — we don't have retirement accounts or large portfolios. Still, learning how the stock market works matters more than it seems, because it shapes wealth, inequality, and opportunity over decades, including yours.

What owning a stock actually means

A stock is a small share of ownership in a company. When you buy one share of Apple, you own a tiny claim on its future profits. If the company grows and earns more, that claim becomes more valuable. If it fails to adapt or makes bad decisions, it shrinks. Behind every ticker symbol is a real business with workers, products, and customers. The market's job is to continuously update what those future earnings are worth today, based on all available information.

Why prices move so much

Stock prices react to expectations, not just current results. A company can report strong profits and still see its share price fall if investors expected even better numbers. Interest rates, inflation, political events, and technological shifts all feed into these expectations constantly. This makes the market volatile in the short run — it can feel completely irrational when a supposedly safe stock drops 10% on one piece of news. Over decades, though, broad markets tend to follow the real economy: if businesses innovate and productivity grows, corporate profits grow, and index values rise with them.

The long-run compounding argument

This is where it gets genuinely interesting for students. The chart below shows an indexed version of S&P 500 total returns over 30 years, starting at 100 in 1996. Despite multiple crashes — the dot-com bust, 2008, COVID — the long-run trend is unmistakably upward.

S&P 500 total return index, 1996–2026 (1996 = 100)
Despite the dot-com crash, 2008 financial crisis, and COVID collapse, a patient investor who stayed in the market from 1996 to 2026 saw their index grow 4.55x. The crashes are visible — but so is the recovery from every single one.
$1,000 invested in an S&P 500 index fund in 1996 would be worth roughly $4,550 today in real terms — without picking a single stock or making a single trade. That's the power of compounding over time.

Why index funds beat stock-picking

Most professional fund managers cannot consistently beat the market average over long periods. The evidence for this is overwhelming. Index funds — which simply own a little of every company in an index like the S&P 500 — tend to outperform active stock-pickers over time, with lower fees and no guesswork required. For students who eventually want to start investing, a low-cost index fund is almost always the right starting point.

The honest caveat

The market isn't a cure-all. When stock prices rise, people who already own assets benefit most. When financial crises hit, ordinary workers can lose jobs even if they never owned a share. That asymmetry matters for understanding inequality. But ignoring the stock market doesn't make its influence disappear. For students, the goal isn't to start day-trading or treating investing like a game. It's to recognize that patient ownership of productive assets over time is one of the most powerful wealth-building forces available to ordinary people — and to be ready to use that knowledge when the opportunity comes.
