The unemployment rate says "we're fine." My friends don't agree.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

The headline comes out: 3.8% unemployment, near historic lows. Everyone in Washington acts like the economy is basically fixed. I look around at people I know and that number doesn't match anything I'm seeing. Friends' parents getting laid off. Recent graduates taking jobs way below what they trained for. People working three gigs trying to piece together something that feels stable. So what's actually going on?

The official number is measuring the wrong thing

The U-3 unemployment rate, which is the number that makes headlines, only counts people who don't have a job and are actively searching for one. If you got rejected so many times you stopped applying, you're not unemployed according to the government. You just don't exist in the data. If you want 40 hours but can only get 20, you count as employed. If you're doing DoorDash because nothing better has come through, you're employed. The number answers one very narrow question: does this person have any job at all? It says nothing about whether that job is enough to live on.

U-3 asks: does this person have a job? U-6 asks: is this person's work situation actually okay? Those are very different questions and they produce very different numbers.

The number economists actually watch

U-6 is the broader measure. It counts underemployed workers who want more hours and people who've stopped looking but would still take a job if one appeared. It runs consistently about 3 percentage points higher than U-3. That gap is not noise. That gap is millions of people.

That gap never fully closed even in the strongest labor market in decades. Millions of people live in the space between those two lines, underemployed or no longer being counted.

A tighter job market with weaker paychecks

After COVID, wages actually rose for a while, especially at the lower end. Then inflation hit and wiped most of that out. When wages go up 4% but rent goes up 8% and groceries go up 10%, you're falling behind even though on paper you got a raise. The unemployment rate doesn't capture any of that. It just sees: job. Check.

Why this matters for people our age

We're about to enter this labor market. If the only number we look at is 3.8% we'll think everything is fine and be confused when it doesn't feel that way. The real picture is more complicated. Jobs exist but a lot of them don't pay enough to live where they're located, don't offer enough hours, or don't match people's skills. That's worth understanding before we make decisions about what to study, where to live, and what to push for politically. A low unemployment rate is a starting point, not a conclusion.
