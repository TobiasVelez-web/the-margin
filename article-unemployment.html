The unemployment rate says "we're fine." My friends don't agree.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

The headline comes out every month: unemployment at 3.8%, near historic lows, the labor market remains strong. Politicians treat it as evidence the economy is basically fixed. I look around at people I know and that number does not match what I see. Friends' parents laid off after decades at the same company. Recent graduates taking jobs well below their qualifications and calling themselves fortunate. People stringing together two part-time positions because nothing stable has come through. I started trying to understand the gap between the official story and what people are actually experiencing, and the answer is mostly about what the official number is designed to measure.

The number is answering a narrower question than people assume

The U-3 unemployment rate, the figure that appears in headlines, counts only people who do not currently have a job and are actively searching for one. If you applied for months, got no responses, and stopped looking, you are no longer unemployed by this definition. You have simply dropped out of the count. If you want full-time work but can only find 20 hours a week, you are counted as fully employed. If you are driving for a rideshare app because nothing in your field has materialized, you are employed. The statistic is answering one precise question: does this person have any paid work at all? It says nothing about whether that work is sufficient, whether it uses their training, or whether it represents the kind of economic stability the headline implies.

U-3 asks: does this person have a job? U-6 asks: is this person's work situation actually adequate? Those are different questions and they consistently produce numbers about 3 percentage points apart. The gap is not statistical noise. It represents millions of people whose situation the headline number renders invisible.

The measure economists actually watch

U-6 is the Bureau of Labor Statistics' broadest measure of labor underutilization. It counts everyone U-3 counts, plus two additional populations: people who are working part-time but want and are available for full-time work, and people who have stopped searching but would still take a job if one appeared. Right now U-6 sits around 7.1%, compared to the 3.8% headline. That 3.3-point gap has persisted consistently through the strongest labor market in decades.

That gap never fully closed, even at the peak of the strongest labor market in decades. Millions of people live in the space between those two lines, underemployed or no longer counted at all.

A tighter job market with thinner real paychecks

After COVID, wages rose meaningfully for a period, especially at the lower end of the income distribution. For a moment it looked like the labor market might actually compress wage inequality. Then inflation arrived and erased most of those gains in purchasing power terms. When wages increase 4% but rent climbs 8% and groceries climb 10%, you are falling behind in real terms regardless of what your nominal pay does. The unemployment rate captures none of this. It registers that a person has work and moves on.

The composition of job growth matters too, and the headline conceals it. Much of the employment expansion over recent years has been in part-time, gig, and contract arrangements without benefits. A worker who lost a salaried full-time position with health insurance and a 401k match, and replaced the income with two part-time jobs, counts as employed twice in the data. The number says things are fine. Their financial situation says something different.

Why this matters for people our age specifically

We are entering this labor market within the next few years. If 3.8% is the only number we ever check, we will expect one thing and encounter another. Understanding that U-6 runs persistently higher, that wage growth does not automatically translate to real purchasing power gains, and that job counts do not tell you about job quality, changes how you think about what to study, where to locate yourself geographically, and what policies are worth pushing for. A low headline unemployment rate and genuine economic security for workers are not the same condition. Right now they are diverging, and being able to see that divergence clearly requires knowing that the headline statistic was never designed to capture it.
