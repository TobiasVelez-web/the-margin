Labor

The unemployment rate says "we're fine." My friends don't agree.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

You see the headline: "Unemployment at 3.8% — near historic lows." On paper, that sounds like a dream labor market. Almost everyone who wants a job supposedly has one. But when I look around at what students, parents, and recent graduates are actually experiencing, that number does not match reality.

Why the official number is a cropped photo

The official unemployment rate only counts people who are jobless and actively looking for work. If you stopped applying because every resume disappears into a black hole, you are no longer part of the statistic. If you are working part-time but want full-time hours, you are not counted as unemployed. If you are doing gig work while hoping for a stable salary, you are still "employed." The result is a picture that looks stronger than it feels.

The headline figure answers one narrow question: "Do most people have some kind of job?" It does not answer the more important one: "Do those jobs provide stability and enough income to keep up with prices?"

U-3 vs. U-6: the gap that tells the real story

Economists actually track a broader measure called U-6, which includes people working part-time who want full-time work, and people who stopped looking but still want a job. The gap between U-3 and U-6 is where the real labor market lives.

The persistent ~3 percentage point gap between the official rate and the broader U-6 measure represents millions of Americans who are underemployed or have stopped searching. Even in a "strong" labor market, this gap never closes.

A tighter market with weaker security

In theory, low unemployment means workers have bargaining power. That happened briefly after the pandemic — especially in lower-wage service jobs. But as inflation surged, much of that progress was erased. When wages rise a few percent but rent, food, and transportation costs increase faster, real living standards stall even if you technically have a job. The unemployment rate captures whether you are employed. It ignores whether that job can actually support your life.

Why this matters for students

For those of us about to enter the labor market, this gap is a preview. If we only look at U-3, it's easy to assume everything is fine and that anyone struggling is an exception. The broader data suggests something different: a market where many jobs exist, but too many of them are unstable, part-time, or don't pay enough to live near where they're located. Understanding the difference matters when we think about college choices, career paths, and what policies we push for. A low unemployment rate is not meaningless — it's just the beginning of the story.
