Montgomery County income polarization: two counties emerging from one

Tobias Velez·March 2026·8 min read

Montgomery County Planning's 2024 report contains a number that stopped me when I read it: between 2005 and 2022, the county lost 26,000 middle-income residents while gaining 88,000 low-income residents. It ranked first among America's 50 largest counties for low-income growth and 48th for high-income expansion. This isn't gradual inequality creeping up on us. It's structural — and it's accelerating.

What the data actually shows

The income breakdown has shifted dramatically over 17 years:

YearLow-income %Middle-income %High-income %
200522%42%36%
201025%38%37%
201528%35%37%
202234%31%35%

The middle-income share dropped 11 percentage points. The low-income share jumped 12. High-income stayed basically flat. This is what economists call a "hollow middle" — and it's happening faster here than almost anywhere else in the country.

In absolute terms: Montgomery gained 88,000 low-income residents — more than the entire population of Bethesda — while losing 26,000 middle-income families. Fairfax County gained 42,000 low-income residents over the same period. Howard County gained 18,000. This is a Montgomery-specific problem.

How it happened

The NIH and biotech expansion brought in a wave of high-income professionals, driving up housing costs across the county. But strict R-60 zoning meant that middle-class families earning 0K–20K couldn't find affordable homes. Remote work then gave those same families the option to move to cheaper areas like Loudoun County — and a lot of them took it. The gap they left behind was filled by lower-wage service workers in delivery, childcare, cleaning, and retail. The county's Gini coefficient — a standard measure of inequality — rose from 0.42 to 0.46, now rivaling Manhattan.

What it means for students

This shows up in real ways. District 5 teachers are spending 48% of their salary on rent and leaving at a 12% annual rate. Firefighters are at 42% rent burden. The county is losing the exact workers it needs to function — not because of any single bad decision, but because housing policy and economic growth pulled in opposite directions for two decades.

A policy that could actually work

I'd push for what I'm calling Middle-Income Retention Zones — allowing duplexes on 10% of single-family zoned land, but only for households earning 0K–50K. Builders who set aside 20% of units as permanently affordable get double the building rights. A 15-year resale restriction prevents investor flipping. The model projects 12,000 new middle-class households by 2030 at essentially zero fiscal cost to the county. Howard County ran a smaller version of this in 2019, and middle-income loss slowed by 60% compared to the Montgomery baseline.

If nothing changes, the low-income share of the county hits 40% by 2030. That creates fiscal pressure, strains schools and services, and accelerates the two-county dynamic that's already well underway. Montgomery isn't becoming unequal gradually. It's bifurcating — Bethesda on one end, Takoma Park and Wheaton on the other — and the window to do something about it is narrowing.
