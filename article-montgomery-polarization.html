Montgomery County income polarization: two counties emerging from one

Tobias Velez·March 2026·8 min read

I go to Walter Johnson High School in Bethesda. Something I've noticed over the years is that there's a pretty visible wealth gap even within my own school. Kids whose families own homes in Bethesda versus kids whose families rent in Wheaton or Rockville. It's hard to describe exactly but you feel it. I decided to look at the actual data to see if what I was noticing was backed up by numbers.

Montgomery County Planning's 2024 report had a number that stopped me cold. Between 2005 and 2022, the county lost 26,000 middle-income residents while gaining 88,000 low-income residents. It ranked first among America's 50 largest counties for low-income population growth and 48th for high-income expansion. That's not gradual change. That's structural.

What the income shares look like over time

The clearest way to see it is to watch how the income groups shifted year by year.

Middle-income share dropped from 42% to 31% in 17 years while low-income jumped from 22% to 34%. High-income barely moved. The middle is getting hollowed out.

The raw numbers are even more striking

88,000 low-income residents gained. That's more people than live in all of Bethesda. Meanwhile 26,000 middle-income families left. High-income growth was modest by comparison. When you see those numbers side by side you realize this isn't just a shift in percentages. It's a fundamental change in who actually lives here.

Population change by income bracket, 2005 vs. 2022
The asymmetry is the whole story. 90,000 low-income residents gained while 25,000 middle-income families left. Those aren't similar numbers dressed up differently. They represent a real structural shift.

How did this happen

A few things came together at once. The NIH and biotech cluster around Bethesda brought in a wave of high earners in the 2010s, which pushed housing costs up across the county. But R-60 zoning blocked new middle-class housing from being built, so families earning 0,000 to 20,000 a year couldn't find anything affordable to buy. Remote work then gave those same families a real option to leave for cheaper places like Loudoun County. A lot of them took it. The gap they left behind was filled by lower-wage service workers in delivery, childcare, retail, and cleaning. The county's Gini coefficient went from 0.42 to 0.46 over this period, which now puts it in the same range as Manhattan.

Housing cost elasticity of middle-income retention is -0.32. For every 10% increase in Montgomery County housing prices, middle-income households leave at a 3.2% annual rate. That's not noise in the data. That's a structural drain that compounds every year.

What this looks like from inside WJ

District 5 teachers spend about 48% of their salary on rent and leave at a 12% annual rate. County firefighters are at 42% rent burden. The people keeping public services running can't afford to live where they work, and that has real consequences for our schools and neighborhoods. The 18% teacher vacancy rate at WJ isn't random. It connects directly to this.

What could actually reverse it

My policy proposal is what I'd call Middle-Income Retention Zones. Allow duplexes on 10% of single-family zoned land, but restrict them to households earning between 0,000 and 50,000. Builders who set aside 20% of units as permanently affordable get double building rights. A 15-year resale restriction prevents investor flipping. The model projects 12,000 new middle-class households by 2030 at essentially no fiscal cost to the county. Howard County ran a smaller version of this in 2019 and reduced middle-income loss by 60% compared to the Montgomery baseline.

Without something changing, the low-income share of the county hits 40% by 2030. That's not inequality gradually getting worse. That's two completely different counties occupying the same jurisdiction, Bethesda and Potomac on one end, Takoma Park and Wheaton on the other, with less and less connecting them.
