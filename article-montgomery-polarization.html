Inequality

Montgomery County is splitting in two. The data makes it hard to look away.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·9 min read

I go to Walter Johnson in Bethesda. If you go there too, or went there, you probably know what I'm about to describe without needing data to confirm it. There is a wealth gap visible within the school itself. Not just between schools in different parts of the county, but inside the same building, between students whose families own homes here and students whose families rent in Wheaton or Gaithersburg and commute in. It is not something anyone talks about explicitly. You just feel it. The parties at certain houses, the cars in the senior lot, the vacations people mention casually, the way some kids treat summer as something to fill and others treat it as time to work.

I wanted to stop relying on that impression and see whether it was backed by actual numbers. The Montgomery County Planning Department's 2024 report turned out to be one of the more striking documents I have read. Between 2005 and 2022, the county lost 26,000 middle-income residents while gaining 88,000 low-income residents. It ranked first among America's 50 largest counties for low-income population growth during that period. It ranked 48th for high-income expansion. These are not marginal numbers. They describe a county undergoing a structural transformation that is not, as far as I can tell, part of any intentional policy. It is just happening, driven by housing markets and zoning decisions and economic forces that nobody sat down and chose.

What the income share shift actually looks like

In 2005, middle-income households made up about 42% of Montgomery County's population. By 2022 that had fallen to 31%. The low-income share went from 22% to 34% over the same period. High-income stayed roughly flat. The percentage point changes sound modest until you remember that this is happening in a county of over a million people. An 11-point drop in the middle-income share represents tens of thousands of families who were part of the county's economic backbone and are no longer here.

Middle-income households did not move up into the high-income category. They left the county. The low-income share grew 12 points over 17 years, representing roughly 90,000 more low-income residents.

Ninety thousand people is not a rounding error

I want to sit with the raw numbers for a second because the percentages can make something large feel abstract. Eighty-eight thousand low-income residents gained between 2005 and 2022. That is more people than currently live in all of Bethesda, Chevy Chase, and Potomac combined. Meanwhile 26,000 middle-income families left. Not shifted downward into the low-income category within the county. Left.

High-income growth was modest in comparison, around 25,000 people. So the county is not experiencing a bimodal split where both ends are growing. It is experiencing lopsided growth at the lower end and net outflow in the middle. The Gini coefficient went from 0.42 to 0.46 during this period. That puts Montgomery County in the same income inequality range as Manhattan, which is not what most people picture when they think of a suburban Maryland jurisdiction.

The housing cost elasticity of middle-income retention here is approximately negative 0.32. For every 10% rise in local housing prices, middle-income households leave at a 3.2% annual rate. That rate does not stabilize on its own. It compounds year over year until some external force interrupts it.

The mechanism, in order

This did not happen randomly. Several specific forces converged, and understanding the sequence matters because it points toward which interventions would actually work.

The first phase was demand-side pressure. The NIH expansion and the growth of the biotech corridor around Bethesda brought sustained high-earner in-migration through the late 2000s and 2010s. These are genuinely high-income households, scientists and physicians and federal contractors pulling $200,000 to $400,000 a year, and they bid aggressively for housing in the neighborhoods closest to their workplaces. Prices in Bethesda, Chevy Chase, and Potomac rose sharply.

The second phase was the supply failure. R-60 zoning prevented any construction response to that demand shock. No new duplexes. No new multifamily. No meaningful addition to the housing stock that could have absorbed the price pressure. Instead, the bidding concentrated on a fixed number of units and prices rose to whatever the high-earning newcomers were willing to pay.

The third phase was the exit. Middle-income families, teachers, nurses, county workers, small business owners, people earning $80,000 to $150,000, found that the places they could afford to live were moving further and further from the places they worked. Remote work made the calculation explicit: if you are going to commute an hour anyway, you might as well commute from somewhere cheaper and actually own a home. Loudoun County, Frederick, even Hagerstown. A lot of families did exactly that.

The fourth phase, the one we are living through now, is replacement. The gap left by departing middle-income families has been filled by lower-wage service workers in childcare, elder care, delivery, retail, and domestic work who are themselves getting pushed out of more expensive nearby jurisdictions. This is not a moral observation. It is a description of how labor markets and housing markets interact when you remove the moderating influence of middle-income supply.

What it looks like from inside WJ

District 5 teachers spend about 48% of their take-home salary on housing and turn over at roughly 12% annually. County firefighters average a 42% housing cost burden. These numbers are not aberrations. They are the predictable consequence of a housing market that has priced essential workers out of the communities they serve. The 18% teacher vacancy rate at Walter Johnson connects directly to this. So does the quality of substitute instruction, the institutional knowledge lost when experienced teachers leave, the difficulty of building any continuity in a school program when the staff changes constantly.

This is the part that feels most personal to me. The school I go to every day is visibly affected by a housing market I also just described analytically. Those are not separate things. The vacancy rate is not a school problem. It is a housing problem that shows up in school.

What could actually reverse this

The simplest version of my proposal is what I've been calling Middle-Income Retention Zones. Identify 10% of single-family zoned land countywide and permit duplex construction on it, restricted by deed covenant to households earning between $80,000 and $150,000. Builders who agree to price 20% of units as permanently affordable get expanded development rights. A 15-year resale restriction prevents the units from being quickly flipped into the luxury market. The projected output is around 12,000 new middle-class households in the county by 2030 at no direct fiscal cost to taxpayers.

Howard County implemented a scaled-down version of this approach in 2019 and reduced middle-income population loss by 60% against the Montgomery County baseline over the same subsequent period. That is not a perfect natural experiment but it is meaningful evidence.

The alternative trajectory is a county where the low-income share reaches 40% by 2030, the school system continues losing teachers faster than it can replace them, and the tax base narrows as the middle-income households who generate the most stable property tax and income tax revenue continue leaving. At that point the polarization has compounded to a level where correcting it requires either a decade of sustained policy intervention or an economic shock large enough to reset prices downward. Neither is something a county government can count on. The window to address this with targeted policy is probably not as wide as it looks from the outside.
