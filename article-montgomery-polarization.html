Inequality

Montgomery County income polarization: two counties emerging from one

Tobias Velez·March 2026·8 min read

I go to Walter Johnson in Bethesda. There is a wealth gap visible within the school itself, between students whose families own homes here and students whose families rent in Wheaton or Rockville. It shows up in small, hard-to-name ways. I decided to stop relying on impressions and look at the actual data. The Montgomery County Planning Department's 2024 report had a number that made me put down what I was doing: between 2005 and 2022, the county lost 26,000 middle-income residents while gaining 88,000 low-income residents. It ranked first among America's 50 largest counties for low-income population growth and 48th for high-income expansion. That is not gradual demographic drift. That is structural transformation, and it has been accelerating.

What the income share data shows

In 2005, middle-income households made up about 42% of the county's population. By 2022 that share had fallen to 31%. Over the same period, the low-income share climbed from 22% to 34%. High-income barely moved. The middle is getting hollowed out, and the two ends are pulling further apart each year.

Middle-income share fell 11 percentage points over 17 years while low-income share rose 12 points. High-income barely changed. The middle is not moving upward. It is leaving.

The raw numbers are more striking than percentages

88,000 low-income residents gained over 17 years is more people than currently live in all of Bethesda. Meanwhile 26,000 middle-income families left. When you put those numbers next to each other it stops being a story about income categories shifting and becomes a story about who literally lives here now and who no longer can.

The housing cost elasticity of middle-income retention in Montgomery County is approximately negative 0.32. For every 10% increase in local housing prices, middle-income households leave at a 3.2% annual rate. That compounds. It does not stabilize on its own.

How this happened

Several forces converged at once. The NIH and biotech cluster around Bethesda brought sustained high-earner in-migration through the 2010s, pushing housing costs well above what middle-income families could absorb. R-60 zoning prevented any supply response, so families earning $80,000 to $120,000 a year found nothing affordable even when they wanted to stay. Remote work then gave those same families a real exit option: Loudoun County, Frederick, even further. A significant number took it.

The gap they left got filled by lower-wage service workers in delivery, childcare, retail, and domestic labor who were themselves pushed into Montgomery County by rising costs elsewhere in the region. The county's Gini coefficient moved from 0.42 to 0.46 over this period. That range puts Montgomery County in the same inequality tier as Manhattan, which is not an association most people picture when they think of suburban Maryland.

What this looks like inside WJ

District 5 teachers spend roughly 48% of their salary on housing and leave at a 12% annual rate. County firefighters are at a 42% housing cost burden. The professionals keeping public services running cannot afford to live in the community they serve. The 18% teacher vacancy rate at Walter Johnson is not a hiring failure. It is a housing market failure that shows up in classrooms. Every unfilled position is a direct downstream effect of a zoning regime that made it economically impossible for the people who hold essential jobs to live near those jobs.

What could reverse it

My policy proposal is what I'd call Middle-Income Retention Zones. Allow duplexes on 10% of single-family zoned land, restricted to households earning between $80,000 and $150,000. Builders who set aside 20% of units as permanently affordable receive double building rights. A 15-year resale restriction prevents investor capture. The projected outcome is 12,000 new middle-class households by 2030 at no net fiscal cost to the county. Howard County implemented a smaller version of this in 2019 and reduced middle-income population loss by 60% against the Montgomery County baseline.

Without intervention of some kind, the low-income share of the county reaches 40% by 2030. That is not inequality getting gradually worse. That is two functionally separate places occupying the same jurisdiction, Bethesda and Potomac on one end, Wheaton and Takoma Park on the other, with a shrinking middle and almost nothing connecting them economically or socially. The county's tax base, its school quality, its civic identity, all of those depend on reversing a trend that the data shows has been running for nearly two decades.
