Montgomery County income polarization: two counties emerging from one

Tobias Velez·March 2026·8 min read

Montgomery County Planning's 2024 report contains a number that stopped me: between 2005 and 2022, the county lost 26,000 middle-income residents while gaining 88,000 low-income residents. It ranked first among America's 50 largest counties for low-income growth and 48th for high-income expansion. This isn't gradual inequality creeping up on us. It's structural bifurcation — and it's accelerating.

Chart 1: Income group shares over time

The most striking way to see what's happening is to watch the income shares shift year by year. Notice how the middle-income gray area shrinks 11 percentage points while low-income expands dramatically. High-income stays basically flat.

The middle-income share collapsed 11 points (42% → 31%) while low-income surged 12 points (22% → 34%). High-income barely moved. This is the "hollow middle" pattern playing out in real time.

Chart 2: Absolute population flows

Percentages can hide the scale. In raw numbers, Montgomery gained 88,000 low-income residents — more than the entire population of Bethesda — while losing 26,000 middle-income families. High-income growth was modest by comparison.

Population change by income bracket, 2005 vs. 2022
Montgomery gained 90,000 low-income residents — more than the entire population of Bethesda — while losing 25,000 middle-income families. The asymmetry is the story.

Chart 3: Regional polarization index

Montgomery isn't just polarizing — we're leading the region on both extremes simultaneously. Compared to Fairfax and Howard Counties, our combination of high low-income growth and high middle-income loss is uniquely severe.

Montgomery leads the region on both extremes — highest low-income gain (90K) and highest middle-income loss (25K). Fairfax and Howard County aren't even close. This is a Montgomery-specific crisis, not a regional trend.

How it happened

The NIH and biotech expansion brought in a wave of $200K+ earners, driving up housing costs across the county. But strict R-60 zoning meant families earning $80K–$120K couldn't find affordable homes to buy. Remote work then gave those families the option to leave for cheaper places like Loudoun County — and many took it. The gap they left behind was filled by lower-wage service workers in delivery, childcare, cleaning, and retail. The county's Gini coefficient rose from 0.42 to 0.46 — now rivaling Manhattan.

Housing cost elasticity of middle-income retention = -0.32. For every 10% increase in Montgomery housing prices, middle-income households exit at a 3.2% rate. That's not a rounding error — it's a structural drain.

What it means for students

This shows up in ways we actually feel. District 5 teachers spend 48% of their salary on rent and leave at a 12% annual rate. County firefighters are at 42% rent burden. The people who keep the county running can't afford to live here anymore — and that has real consequences for our schools, our neighborhoods, and the kind of county we inherit.

A policy that could reverse this

My proposal: Middle-Income Retention Zones (MIRZ) — allowing duplexes on 10% of single-family land, restricted to households earning $80K–$150K. Builders who set aside 20% of units as permanently affordable get double building rights. A 15-year resale restriction prevents investor flipping. The model projects 12,000 new middle-class households by 2030 at essentially zero fiscal cost. Howard County ran a smaller version of this in 2019 and reduced middle-income loss by 60% compared to the Montgomery baseline. Without intervention, the low-income share hits 40% by 2030. The window is narrowing.
