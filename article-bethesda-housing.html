Bethesda housing crisis: why prices just won't fall

Tobias Velez·March 2026·7 min read

Bethesda median home prices hit .2 million in 2025. That's up 142% since 2010. When mortgage rates spiked to 7% in 2022 and 2023, housing markets across the country finally started cooling. Bethesda barely moved. I wanted to understand why, so I dug into BrightMLS data and Montgomery County Planning reports. The answer isn't that complicated, but it does require looking at a few things together.

There just aren't enough homes for sale

The average home in Bethesda sells in about 21 days. Nationally that number is closer to 45. Even when rates were at their highest, sellers here had almost zero reason to lower their prices because there was barely anything else on the market. In 2025 only 41 new listings were hitting the market each month while 69 homes were closing. That works out to 0.6 months of inventory.

A normal balanced real estate market has somewhere between 4 and 6 months of inventory. Bethesda is at 0.6. At that level prices don't just stay flat, they keep compounding upward regardless of what interest rates are doing nationally.
Bethesda vs. national median days on market, 2020-2025
Bethesda homes sell in roughly half the time of the national average. Even during the 2022-2023 rate spike when national days-on-market jumped significantly, Bethesda barely budged.

Why nobody is selling

About 67% of Bethesda homeowners locked in mortgages at around 3% between 2019 and 2021. Their monthly payment on a typical home might be around ,500. If they sell today and try to buy something comparable at 6.5% rates, that same payment jumps to roughly ,800. So they stay put. And since they stay put, there's nothing for anyone else to buy. It's a trap that reinforces itself every year rates stay elevated.

Zoning makes it worse

R-60 zoning covers 78% of Bethesda's residential land. What that means practically is you can build a single-family home and basically nothing else. No duplexes, no townhomes, no small apartment buildings. The supply elasticity here is 0.08, meaning for every 100 new households wanting to move in, only 8 new homes can legally get built. The national average is 0.45. The math doesn't work when rules prevent supply from responding to demand.

Who actually pays for this

This is where it stops being abstract. Walter Johnson's teacher vacancy rate hit 18% in 2025. A first-year teacher making 8,000 is spending close to half their income on rent in this area. That's not sustainable and it shows up in classrooms. Firefighters, nurses, young professionals trying to start out here face the same math. The people the county needs most are the ones who can least afford to stay.

What would actually help

My proposal is a targeted public land program where the county purchases 50 tear-down lots per year and builds duplexes reserved for first-time buyers earning between 20,000 and 00,000. Teachers, firefighters, young professionals. Each lot that would have become one luxury home instead creates six middle-income ones. Arlington County ran something similar from 2019 to 2024 and added 4,200 units while cutting teacher vacancies by 42%.

The bigger fix is rezoning. If Bethesda allowed duplexes on just 5% of R-60 land, the models suggest days-on-market rises to around 35, annual price growth slows from 12% toward 4%, and teacher vacancies start dropping. Without any policy change the projection is .8 million median prices by 2030. At that point it's not really a housing market. It's just a place most people can't afford to live.
