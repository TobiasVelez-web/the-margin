Why concert tickets cost so much — and why that's actually rational

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

You refresh Ticketmaster. A Lil Baby ticket shows $85. You wait two hours in the queue, and suddenly it's $340. You feel robbed. But here's the economic reality: nothing unfair actually happened. The market found its true price.

Supply and demand 101

Arenas have a fixed supply — 20,000 seats maximum. Lil Baby can't clone himself for more shows. When 500,000 fans compete for those seats, prices must rise until the number of buyers willing to pay matches the available inventory. The chart below makes this concrete.

Buyers willing to pay at each price point (20,000 seats available)
At $340, buyers willing to pay (20,000) exactly equals the number of seats available — that's the equilibrium. Below it, demand massively outstrips supply. Above it, seats go unsold.

Dynamic pricing: not new, just honest

Airlines have done this forever. Friday night Ubers surge 3x. Hotels charge $500 a night during Coachella. Ticketmaster just made it real-time and visible. The algorithm watches queue length, time to show, seat location, and remaining inventory — and adjusts prices as demand shifts. As inventory drops from 20,000 to 2,000 seats, prices adjust upward accordingly.

The scalper alternative — and why it's worse for everyone

Here's what actually happens without dynamic pricing: Ticketmaster sells at $85 to the first 20,000 buyers. Scalpers, using bots, grab the bulk of those tickets instantly. Fans end up paying $400+ on StubHub anyway — except now the artist gets $85 and the scalper pockets $315. With dynamic pricing: artist gets $340, fans pay market rate, no middleman profits from the chaos. The money goes to the people who actually made the show happen.

Why it still feels wrong — even when it isn't

Economists call this price gouging resentment. Our brains anchor to the first number we see — $85 — and the jump to $340 violates our sense of what's "fair," even when the market logic is completely sound. I get it. I wanted Bad Bunny floor seats last year. They started at $120 and hit $550 after 90 minutes. It felt like theft. But 200,000 people from the DMV wanted those 300 floor seats. Someone was going to pay $550. Better the artist gets it than a scalper with a bot farm.

My take

Dynamic pricing is the more honest system. Fixed cheap tickets are basically a scalper welfare program. What I'd actually push for: hard caps on service fees (those are genuinely ridiculous), stronger anti-bot laws so real fans get a fair shot at the queue, and artist revenue transparency so fans know exactly where their money goes. The price itself isn't the problem. The hidden fees and rigged queues are.
