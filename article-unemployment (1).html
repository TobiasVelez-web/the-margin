The unemployment rate says "we're fine." My friends don't agree.

Tobias Velez·March 2026·5 min read

You see the headline: "Unemployment at 3.8% — near historic lows." On paper, that sounds like an incredible labor market. Almost everyone who wants a job supposedly has one. But when I look around at what students, parents, and recent graduates are actually experiencing, that number does not match the vibe.

Why the official number is a cropped photo

The official unemployment rate only counts people who are jobless and actively looking for work. If you stopped applying because every resume feels like it disappears into a black hole, you are no longer part of the statistic. If you are working part-time but want full-time hours, you are not counted as unemployed. If you are doing gig work while hoping for a stable salary, you are still considered "employed." The result is a labor market picture that looks stronger than it actually feels.

The headline figure answers one narrow question: "Do most people have some kind of job?" It doesn't answer the more important one: "Do those jobs provide stability and enough income to keep up with prices?"

A tighter market with weaker security

In theory, low unemployment means workers have bargaining power. That happened briefly after the pandemic — especially for lower-wage service jobs. But as inflation surged, much of that progress got erased. When wages rise a few percent but rent, food, and transportation costs increase faster, real living standards stall even if you technically still have a job. The unemployment rate captures whether you are employed. It ignores whether that job can actually support your life.

U-6 includes people working part-time who want full-time work, and people who have stopped looking but still want a job. That persistent gap of roughly 3 percentage points is the difference between the headline and the reality. Even in a supposedly strong labor market, millions of people are stuck in unstable or mismatched work.

Why this matters for students

For those of us who are about to enter the labor market, this gap is a preview. If we only look at U-3, it is easy to assume everything is fine and anyone struggling is an exception. The broader data tells a different story: a labor market where many jobs exist, but too many of them are part-time, gig-based, or don't pay enough to live near where they are located. Understanding this matters when we think about college choices, career paths, and what kinds of policies we push for. A low unemployment rate is not meaningless — it is just the beginning of the story.
