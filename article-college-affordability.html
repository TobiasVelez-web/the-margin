I'm applying to college right now. Is it still worth it?

Tobias Velez·March 2026·7 min read

I looked up tuition prices seriously for the first time this year. The average private college costs about $58,000 annually. State schools are clearing $30,000. Multiply either of those by four and you have a financial commitment in the range of $120,000 to $230,000, and the expectation is that 17-year-olds will evaluate it thoughtfully with essentially no financial literacy education and a hard deadline. I decided to actually look at the economics before I committed to anything. What I found is complicated in useful ways.

How it got this expensive

Tuition has risen roughly 8 times faster than general inflation since 1980. That is not an accident and it is not one cause. Three distinct mechanisms drove it, and understanding them matters because they point toward which solutions would actually work.

The federal student loan system decoupled price from ability to pay. When buyers can always borrow more, sellers charge more. Once the government made large loans available to essentially any enrolled student regardless of field of study or repayment prospects, colleges discovered they could raise tuition without losing applicants. The loans showed up and covered it. What normally constrains prices in a market, the buyer's ability to pay, was replaced by a debt instrument. That structural change did more to inflate tuition than any other single factor.

U.S. News rankings created incentives that reward spending rather than outcomes. Schools compete on research budgets, campus amenities, selectivity ratios, and faculty prestige. Building a state-of-the-art recreation center moves you up in rankings. Cutting tuition by 20% and improving post-graduation employment rates does not. So schools spent aggressively on the things that affect rankings and passed the cost to students.

Administrative growth compounded everything. The ratio of non-teaching administrators to students at American universities roughly doubled since 1990. More compliance staff, more vice provosts for student experience, more layers of management disconnected from instruction. Those salaries come from tuition revenue, and they have grown faster than teaching staff costs every year for three decades.

Tuition grew more than twice as fast as everything else in the economy for 46 years. The gap between those two lines is paid entirely by students and their families.
The average college graduate in 2026 leaves with about $37,000 in student loan debt. At standard federal interest rates and a 10-year repayment term, that is roughly $370 a month before rent, food, or any other fixed expense. Whether that is manageable depends almost entirely on what you studied and where you work afterward.

Does a degree actually pay off

On average, yes. Bachelor's degree holders earn about 65% more over their lifetimes than workers with only a high school diploma. But that average conceals an enormous range, and the averages have been diverging by field. The relevant question is not whether college pays off in general. It is whether your specific degree from your specific school at your specific price pays off for you.

Those payback periods assume full-price tuition. They compress dramatically if you reduce the cost. A humanities degree from a school where you paid $80,000 total looks very different from the same degree at $230,000. The field and the cost are both variables, and most people only think carefully about one of them.

The options people undervalue

Community college followed by transfer is probably the most financially rational path that gets the least attention. Average tuition is around $4,000 a year. You complete general education requirements locally, transfer to a four-year institution with two years remaining, and graduate with the same credential. The diploma does not indicate where you started. Nobody checks, and in most fields nobody cares. The realistic savings compared to starting at a four-year private school run $60,000 to $100,000.

In-state public universities cost roughly half what comparable private schools charge, and for most careers the institution's name matters considerably less than what you actually do there. The research on name-brand premium in hiring is mixed outside of a narrow set of elite professional programs and specific industries. Financial aid offers are negotiable more often than students realize. If a competing school offers you more, your first-choice school will often match or improve their offer if you ask directly.

How to actually think about this decision

The question worth asking is not "is college worth it?" That framing treats a $120,000 to $230,000 decision as if it has one answer for all people in all situations. The more precise questions are: what is the total cost after all aid, what do graduates from this program actually earn and in what timeframe, what is the loan payment on the debt I would carry, and does that math work given what I want to do? Those are answerable questions. The general one is not.

The fact that society expects 17-year-olds to make this decision without any formal financial literacy education is, taken seriously, its own significant policy failure. The mechanism that inflated the cost and the mechanism that obscures it from the people bearing it are operating simultaneously. Understanding both is not pessimism. It is the only basis for making the decision with clear eyes.
