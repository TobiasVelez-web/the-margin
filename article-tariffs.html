How tariffs make everything more expensive — including your next car

Tobias Velez·March 2026·6 min read

When I started looking at car prices this year, I noticed something surprising — even before you choose options or financing, prices have jumped sharply. According to Kelley Blue Book, the average new car price in early 2026 is about $48,500, compared to $45,000 in 2024 — an increase of nearly 8% in two years. One key reason: higher import costs from new U.S. tariffs.

What a tariff really is

A tariff is a tax on imported goods. When the U.S. imposes a 25% tariff on Canadian steel, it's not Canada paying — it's American importers, who pass most of that cost along to manufacturers and ultimately to consumers. Tariffs raise the price of whatever product depends on imported materials, from cars and electronics to clothes and groceries.

The common misconception is that tariffs hurt the foreign country. In reality, economists at the Peterson Institute for International Economics found that U.S. consumers and businesses absorb roughly 90% of tariff costs — not the exporters being targeted.

What's happening right now (2025–2026)

The current administration imposed sweeping new tariffs starting in late 2025:

  • 25% tariffs on goods from Canada and Mexico, including steel, dairy, and auto parts
  • 10%–145% tariffs on Chinese goods, especially electronics, solar panels, and electric vehicles
  • The USTR estimates these tariffs cover over $400 billion worth of imported goods

Trade partners didn't sit still. China imposed 20%–50% tariffs on U.S. soybeans, lithium batteries, and autos. Canada added 10% tariffs on U.S. alcohol, household goods, and farm products. The EU targeted U.S. cars and bourbon with tariffs up to 25%. That's a trade war — and both sides' consumers pay for it.

Who actually pays

Who pays for U.S. tariffs?
Of the $110 billion in tariff revenue collected in 2025, roughly $95 billion was effectively paid by American consumers and businesses through higher prices — not by foreign exporters.

Lessons from the last trade war

This isn't the first time the U.S. has gone down this road. During the 2018–2019 tariffs, economists found nearly identical outcomes: tariffs covered $360 billion in goods, U.S. consumers bore about 90% of the cost, and China's exports to the U.S. fell 25% — but imports from non-tariffed countries like Vietnam rose 30%, offsetting much of the impact. The biggest casualty was American agriculture: soybean and corn exports to China plunged nearly 40% after retaliation, prompting $28 billion in emergency federal farm aid.

Are tariffs ever worth it?

Supporters argue tariffs protect American jobs and pressure countries like China to play fair on trade. There's some truth to that — certain strategic industries like semiconductors and steel genuinely benefit from protection, and some economists argue the long-term gains outweigh short-term consumer costs. Critics counter that tariffs act like hidden taxes on ordinary people. Studies from the CBO and Brookings Institution show that sustained tariffs can reduce GDP by 0.3–0.5 percentage points and slightly raise inflation. The honest answer is: it depends on the industry, the duration, and whether the other side actually changes its behavior.

Why this matters to us

As students, many of these effects hit things we actually save for or buy soon — our first car, our phone, clothes, groceries for college. Tariffs might sound like an abstract trade policy debate, but they're part of why our daily expenses keep climbing. Every decision made in global trade negotiations eventually shows up at the checkout counter. Understanding that connection is the first step to actually having an informed opinion about it.
